Engineering Deep Dive: Enclosing Heat-Generating Equipment

The physics of how to enclose a heater or chiller without increasing device temperature or reducing efficiency.

Critical Design Principle: Heat pumps, pool chillers, and heaters generate significant heat that MUST be removed. An improperly designed enclosure will cause the equipment to overheat, reduce efficiency, trigger safety shutdowns, or cause permanent damage.

Understanding Heat Pump Thermodynamics

Pool chillers and heat pumps work by moving heat from one place to another using a refrigeration cycle.

Heat Pump Operating Principle

The Physics of Enclosure Design

Problem: Hot Air Recirculation

If hot exhaust air (45°C+) recirculates back to the intake, the chiller's efficiency drops dramatically.

Effect: Every 1°C increase in inlet air = 2-3% efficiency loss.

10°C recirculation = 20-30% efficiency loss + potential compressor damage.

Solution: Separated Airflow Paths

Design the enclosure so intake and exhaust air never mix:

- Intake from sides/bottom (cool ambient air)
- Exhaust from top/opposite side (hot air rises)
- Minimum 500mm separation between paths

How to Enclose Without Overheating

Correct Enclosure Airflow Design

Design Rules for Heat-Generating Equipment Enclosures

RuleSpecificationReasoning
1. Minimum Open Area ≥50% on inlet and exhaust sides Ensures airflow is not restricted below equipment needs
2. Louver Angle 30-45° downward Sheds rain while allowing airflow; reduces noise transmission
3. Intake/Exhaust Separation ≥500mm vertical or horizontal Prevents hot exhaust recirculating to intake
4. Clearance from Equipment ≥300mm all sides (check manufacturer) Allows air to flow around unit; service access
5. Top Exhaust Path Open or louvered top, never sealed Hot air rises naturally; must have exit path
6. No Dead Corners Air must flow through, not trap Stagnant air pockets cause local overheating

Calculating Required Airflow

Engineering Formula:

CFM = (Heat Rejection in BTU/hr) ÷ (1.08 × ΔT)

Where ΔT = allowable temperature rise (typically 10-15°F / 5-8°C)

Example: A 10kW chiller rejecting 34,000 BTU/hr with 10°F rise:
CFM = 34,000 ÷ (1.08 × 10) = 3,148 CFM required

This is why chillers have large fans and CANNOT be fully enclosed.

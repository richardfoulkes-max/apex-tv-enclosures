Acoustic Design Guide

Technical principles for soundproofing pool heat pumps with ventilation

1. The Problem: Heat Pump Noise

Pool heat pumps typically produce 55-65 dB(A) at 1 metre distance. In quiet residential settings, this can be intrusive, especially during evening use when ambient noise is lower.

2. Acoustic Principles

Mass Barrier

Heavy, dense materials block sound transmission. Our 2mm aluminium walls provide ~20 dB reduction at mid-frequencies.

Absorption

Porous materials convert sound energy to heat. 50mm mineral wool (NRC 0.85) absorbs sound inside the enclosure.

Path Length

Sound loses energy over distance. Labyrinth paths force sound through multiple turns, each reducing level by 3-6 dB.

Decoupling

Vibration isolation prevents structure-borne sound. Rubber mounts isolate the enclosure from ground transmission.

3. The Ventilation Challenge

Heat pumps require continuous airflow (2000-6000 m³/hr) to operate efficiently. Blocking airflow causes equipment damage, but openings leak sound. The solution is acoustic louvres - ventilation paths designed to attenuate sound while allowing air passage.

Trade-off: Deeper blades = more attenuation but less airflow. The APE-HC uses 100mm depth as the optimal balance for pool heat pumps.

4. APE-HC Acoustic Strategy

Multi-Layer Approach

Layer Component Attenuation Cumulative
1 Acoustic louvre (intake) 12-15 dB 12-15 dB
2 Internal absorption (walls) 3-5 dB 15-20 dB
3 Acoustic louvre (exhaust) 12-15 dB 27-35 dB
4 Mass barrier (flanking paths) 15-20 dB -
Practical Result 15-20 dB overall reduction
Why not 35 dB? Real-world performance is limited by flanking paths (sound leaking around barriers), imperfect seals, and low-frequency content that's hard to attenuate with compact louvres. Our 18 dB target is achievable and meaningful.

6. Low Frequency Considerations

Compressor hum (100-250 Hz) is the most challenging frequency to attenuate. At these wavelengths (1.4 - 3.4 metres), thin barriers and small louvres are less effective.

Low Frequency Mitigation

  • Mass: 2mm aluminium walls help block low frequencies (mass law)
  • Vibration isolation: Rubber mounts prevent structure-borne transmission
  • Distance: Position enclosure away from bedrooms/living areas
  • Expectations: Accept ~10 dB reduction at low frequencies vs 18 dB at mid/high
Realistic Expectations: If the heat pump produces a prominent 125 Hz hum at 58 dB, expect reduction to ~48 dB - audible but not intrusive. For extreme low frequency problems, consider a concrete barrier wall in addition to the enclosure.

7. Testing & Validation

Recommended Test Protocol

  1. Measure heat pump noise without enclosure (1m distance, A-weighted)
  2. Install enclosure, repeat measurement at same position
  3. Calculate insertion loss (difference)
  4. Check at multiple angles (front, side, rear)
  5. Verify equipment operating temperature is within spec
